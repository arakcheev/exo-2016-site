    Luca Fossati (Space Research Institute, Austrian Academy of Sciences, Austria ) A comprehensive overview of results obtained from multi-wavelength exoplanet transit observations

    The study of extra-solar planets (exoplanets) is arguably the most exciting and fastest-growing field in Astrophysics. We are only now beginning to see and understand the large variety of exoplanets, starting to classify them on the basis of their properties. Observations of transiting close-in planets at ultraviolet (UV) wavelengths revealed that such planets are subject to powerful mass-loss that shapes planetary structure, composition, and evolution. Optical and infrared transit observations revealed for example that clouds and hazes are common features of planetary atmospheres. I will review the past observations and advances in exoplanet science obtained on the basis of UV, optical, and infrared observations and discuss the prospects of further discoveries on the basis of the currently available and planned ground- and space-based telescopes.

    Helmut Lammer (Space Research Institute, Austrian Academy of Sciences, Austria) Exoplanets and their systems: a general view

    So far 2100 planets, 1343 planetary systems and 510 multiple planet systems have been discovered by ground- and space-based methods and in the near future observing facilities will become extremely powerful. After the pioneering discovery of the first transiting super-Earth CoRoT-7b by the CoRoT space observatory in 2009, more exoplanets of that category have been discovered by ground-based projects and NASA's Kepler satellite. The first potentially habitable planets have already been discovered around M dwarf stars and recently around a K star. More potentially habitable exoplanets will be discovered in the near future. Because such discoveries will have significant impact on modern planetary science and society as a whole, this presentation will address the following points:

    • statistic of exoplanets (i.e. radius-mass relationships, etc.);
    • is there a typical architecture of planets and/or planetary systems?
    • what is driving planet formation and evolution?
    • how common may be Earth-like exoplanets.
    By dealing within a multi-parameter space, the most relevant observables that can be described with a minimum of complexity to understand planetary evolution will be discussed.

    Herbert Lichtenegger (Space Research Institute, Austrian Academy of Sciences, Austria) Non-thermal escape from planetary atmospheres

    Any atmosphere bound to a planetary body is subject to various loss processes: Depending on the temperature, some part of the atmospheric particles at the outermost boundary of the atmosphere, the exobase, where collisions become negligible, will have velocities exceeding the escape velocity and may thus leave the planet. Besides this thermal evaporation, supra-thermal particles can be generated by non-thermal collisional processes in the thermosphere which are triggered by solar radiation and magnetospheric plasma. These "hot" particles may either escape directly or move along elongated ballistic trajectories far above the exosphere, thereby forming a huge planetary corona. For planets lacking a magnetospheric obstacle, a substantial part of the corona will be exposed to the solar/stellar wind and could thus be picked up by the plasma flow. In the talk, various escape processes will be presented and possible consequences concerning the long-term stability of planetary atmospheres will be discussed.

    Kristina Kislyakova (Space Research Institute, Austrian Academy of Sciences, Austria) Stellar wind interaction with exoplanets

    We study the interactions between close-in exoplanets and their host stars. We investigate the interaction between stellar winds and the extended hydrogen-dominated upper atmospheres of planets using Direct Simulation Monte Carlo (DSMC) method. This method can be an effective tool for studying non-thermal escape from planetary atmospheres, modelling upper planetary atmospheres. Particle codes are an appropriate tool to study the upper atmospheres, because the collisions between the particles in these regions are rare, so that hydrodynamic solutions are not reliable anymore. Beyond the exobase, the particles are moving on ballistic trajectories following gravity. After the hydrogen corona is modelled, the corresponding absorption of the stellar Ly-alpha line can be calculated and compared with the observations. In this talk, I will describe the available observations of exoplanetary atmospheres in the Ly-alpha line and present the modelling results obtained by our group.

    Alexander A. Cherenkov (Institute of Astronomy, Russian Academy of Sciences, Moscow, Russia) The Influence of Coronal Mass Ejections on the Gas Dynamics of the Exoplanet Atmosphere

    We present the results of 3D numerical gas-dynamic simulations of the interaction between a hot Jupiter atmosphere and a coronal mass ejection of its host star. When simulating, we assume that the parameters of stellar wind and CME correspond to those of the Sun. We consider specific flow-patterns that form in two cases of a quasi-closed and closed (but distorted by the planet’s gravity) gaseous envelope of the planet. We show that a typical CME can sweep out the outer regions (located beyond the planet’s Roche lobe) of the asymmetric envelope, which results in a significant increase of the mass-loss rate of the planet’s atmosphere when it passes through the propagating CME. In our simulations, the mass-loss rate of the closed envelope grows by 11 times, and that of the quasi-closed grows by 14 times, when the planet interacts with the CME. We also discuss possible consequences of this enhanced loss of material.

    Valery I. Shematovich (Institute of Astronomy, Russian Academy of Sciences, Moscow) Dissipation of the planetary atmospheres in the Solar and extrasolar planetary systems

    The escape (or dissipation) from a planetary atmosphere of molecules with thermal energies greater than the gravitational binding energy is referred to as thermal escape. Since the prediction of the escape rates by the thermal and non-thermal processes determines the long-term evolution of planetary atmospheres, it has long been a subject of interest in the aeronomy of planetary objects in our Solar system and more recently of interest in understanding the evolution of extrasolar planets. The particular feature that limits the modeling effort is that escape occurs in a rarefied region of an atmosphere, usually referred to as exosphere, where the gas flow is essentially non-equilibrium. In this region, continuum models break down so that molecular kinetic simulations are required to accurately predict the atmospheric flow structure and the escape rates. A detailed description of the kinetic Monte Carlo method used to study thermal (at high temperature) and non-thermal processes determining the rate of dissipation of planetary atmospheres will be presented.

    On all terrestrial planets, the dissipation timescales of primordial atmospheres are short. Although we cannot travel back in time to observe escape from early solar system terrestrial planets, observations and modeling of exoplanetary atmospheres under strong stellar radiation provide excellent opportunities to verify our theoretical understandings on this key process influencing planet evolution.

    Ildar F. Shaikislamov (Institute of Laser Physics SB RAS, Novosibirsk, Russia) MHD simulation of plasma environment of Hot Jupiter HD 209458b

    HD 209458b is probably the best studied Hot Jupiter. It is the first transiting exoplanet for which atmospheric absorption was observed in the resonance lines. The Doppler-shifted absorption in Lyαline and in lines of heavier species gave first indication that HD 209458b has an extended exosphere in a state of continues blow-off and is losing mass by hydrodynamic flow. Such observations stimulated development of theoretical and numerical models. Despite some quantitative differences the general consensus is that HD 209458b upper atmosphere sustains a super-sonic hydrodynamic escape, or a planetary wind, with temperature of about 104 K and velocity of order of 10 km/s, which carries mass-loss of the order of ~1010–1011 g s-1. The analysis revealed that there are many factors involved in the problem such as heating by stellar ionizing radiation, cooling by expansion and radiation, tidal force, radiation pressure, ionization and recombination, hydrogen chemistry, presence of metals in the outflow, boundary conditions at the planet surface and others. The next level of the problem is the planetary magnetic field. It appears that probable in gas giants values of dipole field can significantly alter the structure and suppress the intensity of the planetary wind. The last but not the least factor of the Hot Jupiters plasma environment is a stellar wind. Interacting with the planetary magnetic field and planetary wind it creates a new type of magnetosphere with unique features. Understanding such magnetosphere and its implications for observations remains an actual task of the modern astrophysics and space plasma physics.
